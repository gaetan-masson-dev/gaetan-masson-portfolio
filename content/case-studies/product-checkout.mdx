## Overview

A tourism platform needed a streamlined checkout flow for booking combined packages of accommodation and marine activities (diving, sailing, snorkeling). The existing process required users to book each item separately, leading to high cart abandonment.

## The Challenge

The original checkout process was fragmented:

- Users had to complete separate bookings for accommodation and activities
- Payment happened twice, creating friction and confusion
- No clear overview of total package pricing
- Mobile experience was particularly painful (60% of traffic)
- Cart abandonment rate was 78%

The business goal was clear: create a unified checkout that felt as simple as booking a hotel, even though we were coordinating multiple vendors and complex scheduling.

## Research

I analyzed user behavior through session recordings and conducted interviews with 12 users who had abandoned bookings:

### Key Insights

- Users expected to book everything in one transaction
- Uncertainty about activity timing relative to accommodation created anxiety
- Mobile users struggled with form inputs and date selection
- Users abandoned when surprised by additional fees late in checkout
- Need to compare different activity options without losing accommodation selection

## Design Principles

Based on research, I established core principles:

1. **Transparent pricing**: Show all costs upfront, no surprises
2. **Unified flow**: One cart, one checkout, one payment
3. **Flexibility**: Easy to modify package before payment
4. **Mobile-first**: Optimize for touch and smaller screens
5. **Confidence**: Clear information about timing, policies, and what's included

## Solution

### Unified Cart

Redesigned the cart to show accommodation + activities as a cohesive package:

- Visual timeline showing how activities fit with accommodation dates
- Clear breakdown of costs (accommodation, activities, fees, taxes)
- Easy to add/remove activities without losing progress

### Streamlined Form

Simplified information collection:

- Smart defaults based on accommodation booking (dates, guest count)
- Progressive disclosure of required fields
- Mobile-optimized date picker and input fields
- Save guest information for faster repeat bookings

### Activity Scheduling

Created an intuitive way to schedule activities:

- Visual calendar showing available time slots
- Automatic conflict detection if activities overlap
- Suggestions for optimal activity scheduling based on popular patterns
- Clear indication of last booking time before trip

### Review & Confirmation

Final review step with clear summary:

- Complete itinerary with accommodation and activity schedule
- Total cost breakdown
- Cancellation and modification policies
- What to expect next (confirmation emails, check-in details)

## Mobile Optimization

Given 60% mobile traffic, I paid special attention to mobile experience:

- Large touch targets (minimum 44px)
- Single-column layout to avoid horizontal scrolling
- Native date pickers where available
- Sticky bottom bar with total price and CTA
- Smart keyboard types for different inputs (email, phone, numbers)

## Testing & Iteration

Conducted usability testing with 10 participants (5 mobile, 5 desktop):

### Initial Findings

- Date picker was confusing for activity scheduling
- Users wanted to see photos of activities in the cart
- Pricing breakdown needed to be more prominent
- Mobile form required too much scrolling

### Refinements

- Redesigned activity scheduler with visual day selector
- Added activity thumbnails to cart
- Made pricing sticky on scroll
- Reduced mobile form fields by consolidating related information

## Results

The new checkout flow significantly improved conversion:

- Cart abandonment decreased from 78% to 34%
- Booking completion time reduced from 12 minutes to 4 minutes
- Mobile conversion improved by 156%
- Customer support inquiries about booking process dropped by 65%
- Average booking value increased by 23% (users added more activities)

## Key Learnings

- **Remove uncertainty**: Users need confidence about scheduling, costs, and policies before committing
- **Mobile isn't just smaller desktop**: Touch targets, input types, and interaction patterns need specific consideration
- **One flow, many edge cases**: Complex backend coordination must be hidden behind simple interface
- **Test with real content**: Generic lorem ipsum hid issues that only appeared with actual activity names and descriptions
